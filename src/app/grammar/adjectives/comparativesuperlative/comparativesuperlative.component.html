Comparative Adjectives

Comparative adjectives are used to compare two things. They are used in sentences where two things are compared.

When using comparatives, we generally see the following sentence structure:

Subject + verb + comparative adjective + than + object.

Examples:

  • Her garden is bigger than mine.
  • My computer is faster than his.
  • Jessica is taller than John.
  • Your neighbors are friend

Superlative Adjectives

Superaltive adjectives are used to compare three or more things. These are used to show the upper or lower limit of a quality within the group.

When using superlatives, we generally see the following sentence structure:

Subject + verb + the + superlative adjective.

Examples:

  • Cristiano Ronaldo is the best footballer on earth.
  • They sell the worst hot dogs I have ever eaten.
  • My dog is by far the fattest dog in the neighborhood.
  • I think that this type of metal is the strongest material that we can find.

Forming Comparatives and Superlatives

For adjectives with one or two syllables, we add an -er to the end of comparatives and -est to the end of superlatives.

- If the word ends in a single consonant, then it is usually doubled before adding the suffix -er or -est.

- If the word ends in -y, then we remove the -y and add an -i before we add the suffix -er or -est.

Examples:

Adjective Comparative Superlative
tall taller tallest
fat fatter fattest
big bigger biggest
small smaller smallest
happy happier happiest
busy busier busiest
funny funnier funniest
curly curlier curliest

For adjectives with three or more syllables, we add more before the adjective for comparatives and most before the adjective for superlatives.

Adjective Comparative Superlative
important more important most important
expensive more expensive most expensive
embarrassed more embarrassed most embarrassed

There are a few exceptions to the syllables rule. Here are a few examples where the adjective contains two syllables, but is not formed with an -er or -est ending, but instead adds more or most before the adjective in forming the comparatives and superlatives.

Adjective Comparative Superlative
anxious more anxious most anxious
famous more famous most famous
immense more immense most immense
nervous more nervous most nervous
perfect more perfect most perfect

Finally, there are a few irregular comparatives and superlatives, which do not take either pattern as shown above.

Adjective Comparative Superlative
good better best
bad worse worst
little less least
much more most
far (extent) further furthest
far (distance) farther farthest
